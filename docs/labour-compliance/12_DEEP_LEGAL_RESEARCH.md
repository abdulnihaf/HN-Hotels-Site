## 0. Disclaimer and how to read this document

This is an internal reference compiled from publicly available Acts, Karnataka High Court and Supreme Court rulings, and authoritative legal commentary. It is **not legal advice**. Any high-stakes termination — especially fraud cases involving large losses, or any retrenchment of more than two workmen at a time — must be vetted by a Bangalore labour-law advocate before execution. The research below tells the owner *what to read for*, *what to expect*, *what mistakes blow up cases* — not what to file in court.

Three big-picture rules to internalise before reading anything else:

1. **Procedure beats substance, every single time.** Indian labour courts will reinstate a thief whose dismissal was procedurally defective. They will affirm dismissal of an employee against weak evidence if the procedure was clean. Spend time on the *paperwork*, not on convincing yourself the employee deserves it — you are already convinced; the court isn't.
2. **The "workman" classification is the master switch.** Almost every protection in Indian labour law depends on whether the employee is a "workman" under Section 2(s) of the Industrial Disputes Act, 1947 ("ID Act"). For HN Hotels, **assume every cook, helper, washer, cleaner, waiter, cashier, tea master, and captain is a workman**, even if the contract calls them "associate" or "manager". Calling someone a manager doesn't make them one. (See section 1.)
3. **Three doors, three different rule books.** Misconduct dismissal, performance termination, and cost-cutting retrenchment travel through three different legal frameworks. Do not confuse them. Deciding which door to walk through is the first and most consequential decision in any termination. (See section 2.)

## 1. Who is a "workman"? The threshold question

### 1.1 Section 2(s), ID Act 1947 — the statutory test

A "workman" is any person employed in any industry to do **manual, unskilled, skilled, technical, operational, clerical or supervisory work** for hire or reward, whether the terms of employment are express or implied. The definition expressly includes apprentices and persons dismissed/retrenched in connection with a dispute.

The Section excludes four categories:
- Persons subject to Air Force/Army/Navy Acts
- Police, prison officers
- Persons employed in **mainly managerial or administrative** capacity
- Persons employed in **supervisory capacity drawing wages above ₹10,000/month** *or* who exercise functions mainly of a managerial nature

### 1.2 Applying it to HN Hotels' 32-staff roster

| Role | Likely workman? | Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Cook / Chef de partie | **Yes** | Skilled work, no hire-fire authority |
| Kitchen helper | **Yes** | Manual / unskilled |
| Washer / cleaner / housekeeping | **Yes** | Manual / unskilled |
| Waiter / steward | **Yes** | Operational / manual; serving, not directing |
| Cashier (POS operator) | **Yes** | Clerical work, even if handling money. Money-handling does not equal "supervisory" |
| Tea master (chai-banane-wala) | **Yes** | Skilled manual work |
| Captain (floor) | **Probably yes**, but borderline | If duties are "directing waiters where to place dishes" → workman. If hires/fires/disciplines staff and salary >₹10K → not workman |
| Head chef / Kitchen-in-charge | **Borderline** | If salary >₹10K AND he hires/fires/approves leave → likely not workman. If he just cooks the harder dishes and the owner makes all people decisions → still workman |
| Outlet manager / supervisor | **Likely not workman** if salary >₹10K AND genuine supervisory authority |
| Accountant (back office) | **Likely not workman** if mainly does books, not just data entry |

**Karnataka HC has consistently held** that the **nomenclature in the appointment letter is irrelevant**; what matters is the **dominant nature of work actually performed** (e.g. *Smt. N. Bhuvaneshwari v. Ambuthirtha Power Pvt Ltd* — manager designation rejected because actual duties were not supervisory). The Supreme Court reaffirmed this test most recently in 2024 (*Lenin Kumar Ray v. Express Publications, 2024 INSC* — "workman" classification turns on actual duties, not designation).

### 1.3 Why this matters

If the employee is **NOT** a workman, none of Chapter V-A of the ID Act applies. Termination is governed only by the **employment contract + Karnataka Shops & Commercial Establishments Act 1961** ("Karnataka SE Act"). Disputes go to civil court, not labour court. This is much easier ground for the employer.

If the employee **IS** a workman (the realistic assumption for ~28 of the 32 staff), then:
- ID Act Sections 25F/25G/25N apply for retrenchment
- Sections 11A, 33, 33A apply during disputes
- Standing Orders Act (model orders) apply
- Industrial Tribunal / Labour Court has exclusive jurisdiction
- The employee gets the procedural protections this document spends 6,000 words explaining

**Practical instruction to owner:** treat all wage-earning staff below outlet-manager level as workmen. Build SOPs for that worst case. If a particular cashier's lawyer later argues he was a "manager" because he had keys to the till, fine — that's the cashier's burden, not yours.

## 2. The three doors: misconduct vs. performance vs. retrenchment

The single most important strategic decision: which legal door does this termination walk through?

| Door | Statutory ground | Standard of proof | Mandatory steps | Compensation | Risk if wrong |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Misconduct (dismissal)** | Service Rules / Standing Orders | Preponderance of probabilities (lower than criminal) | Charge-sheet + show cause + domestic enquiry + speaking order | Forfeit notice pay; gratuity payable unless terminated for offences u/s 4(6) of Gratuity Act | Reinstatement + full back-wages + costs |
| **2. Performance (termination simpliciter)** | Contract / Standing Orders / Sec 39 KSE Act | Documented underperformance (not "stigma") | Notice + reasons + 1 month notice or pay; no enquiry needed | 15 days × completed years (retrenchment compensation); statutory dues | Court reclassifies as misconduct → reinstatement |
| **3. Retrenchment (cost-cutting)** | Sec 25F ID Act | Genuine business need | 1 month notice; 15 days × year compensation; notice to Govt; LIFO; Sec 25H reemployment preference | Full statutory retrenchment compensation | "Sham retrenchment" finding → reinstatement |

### Cardinal sin: don't dress up misconduct as retrenchment, or performance as misconduct

Courts call this **"colourable exercise of power"** and unwind it. The most common failures:

- **Dressing misconduct as performance**: owner has CCTV of theft but doesn't want to do an enquiry, so issues a "performance termination" letter. Court reads it as stigmatic dismissal-without-enquiry → reinstatement. (Ref: principles laid down in *Workmen of Sudder Office, Cinnamara v. Management*, 1972 SC.)
- **Dressing misconduct as retrenchment**: pay 15 days × year compensation and call it retrenchment because owner wants to skip enquiry. Court will examine the "real reason" and unwind. (Ref principle: *Anand Bihari v. Rajasthan SRTC*, 1991 SC.)
- **Forced resignation**: owner tells employee "resign or I file FIR for theft". Even if the threat is true, this is **constructive dismissal**. (Ref: *X v. Registrar, MP High Court*, 2022 SC; *Suresh Kumar v. Sainik School Society* HP HC.)

**Default decision tree:**
1. Is there *evidence of theft / fraud / dishonesty*? → **Door 1 (Misconduct).** Run a real enquiry. Don't shortcut.
2. Is the issue *capability / efficiency* with no bad faith? → **Door 2 (Performance).** Build a paper trail over 60–90 days.
3. Is this purely *not enough work / business loss / role no longer needed*? → **Door 3 (Retrenchment).** Pay the statutory compensation correctly.

## 3. The framework Acts (what applies and where it matters)

### 3.1 Karnataka Shops & Commercial Establishments Act 1961 ("KSE Act")

This is the primary state-level employment law for restaurants in Karnataka. Restaurants, hotels, eating houses, and refreshment houses are **expressly covered** (Sec 2(e), Sec 2(s)). Both HE and NCH are KSE Act establishments and must be registered under it.

**Section 39 — Notice of dismissal.**
Verbatim core: *"No employer shall remove or dismiss an employee who has put in service under him continuously for not less than six months, except for a reasonable cause and unless and until one month's previous notice or pay in lieu thereof has been given to him."*

Two important carve-outs:
- **Service less than 6 months** → no notice required. (This is why probation periods are essential — see section 6.)
- **Misconduct proved at an enquiry** → no notice required. (This is why a clean enquiry record is essential — see section 4.)

Section 39(3) gives the employee a **right of appeal** to the prescribed authority (Asst. Labour Commissioner) within 30 days, on the ground that there was no reasonable cause OR no proven misconduct. The appellate authority can order **reinstatement with back-wages**.

**Section 39(2) interaction with longer benefits:** if an employment contract or award provides longer notice or better severance, the contract overrides the Act. Make sure HN Hotels' contracts don't accidentally promise more (e.g. don't promise "3 months' notice" in any letter unless intended).

**Section 40** is the rule-making power; not a substantive provision. (Some commentaries confuse this with a notice provision — it isn't. Karnataka SE Act notice rules sit in Sec 39.)

**Compounding (Sec 30 / Karnataka Rules 1963):** the Labour Officer can compound offences under the Act for ₹1,000–₹2,000 first offence and ₹2,000–₹5,000 for repeat. This is the realistic enforcement reality — most violations get compounded, not prosecuted, unless there's worker death or large-scale fraud. Don't bank on this; it just means a single procedural slip is a cost-of-business event, not an existential threat.

### 3.2 Industrial Disputes Act 1947 ("ID Act")

Applies because both outlets are "industries" within the wide definition affirmed by *Bangalore Water Supply v. A. Rajappa* (1978 SC). The Supreme Court has repeatedly confirmed restaurants are "industries". This brings in:

- **Sec 2(oo)** — definition of retrenchment (means termination "for any reason whatsoever" *other than* punishment, voluntary retirement, superannuation, ill-health, or non-renewal of fixed-term contract). **Critically**, performance-based termination falls within "retrenchment" because it is "termination for a reason other than misconduct".
- **Sec 25B** — continuous service: 240 days worked in 12 months = deemed 1 year continuous service. Burden on workman to prove (per *Range Forest Officer v. S.T. Hadimani*, 2002 SC), but easily met where attendance registers exist.
- **Sec 25F** — pre-retrenchment conditions (see section 8).
- **Sec 25G** — LIFO (see section 8.4).
- **Sec 25H** — preference in re-employment (see section 8.5).
- **Sec 25-O / 25-N** — closure / mass-retrenchment with prior Govt permission. **Trigger threshold: 100 workmen.** HN Hotels is well below; Sec 25N does NOT apply. This is significant — HN can retrench up to 99 workmen with just Sec 25F compliance, no Govt permission needed.
- **Sec 11A** — Tribunal's power to interfere with disciplinary findings. The Tribunal can re-appraise evidence and substitute its own punishment. This is why even a clean enquiry can be unwound; the procedure has to be more than clean — the *findings* must be reasonable.
- **Sec 33 / 33A** — when a dispute is pending, the employer cannot alter conditions or dismiss the workman without Tribunal approval. Practical impact on HN: if a dispute is filed by anyone, all subsequent terminations need Tribunal approval. Avoid stacking terminations during pending disputes.

### 3.3 Industrial Employment (Standing Orders) Act 1946

**Karnataka threshold: 50 or more workmen** (Karnataka Act 37 of 1975 reduced central threshold of 100 to 50). HN Hotels at 32 employees is **below** the threshold — direct certification is not mandatory.

But two important wrinkles:
- The **Karnataka Industrial Employment (Standing Orders) Rules 1961** publish "model standing orders" which courts apply *as guidance* even to sub-threshold establishments. So HN's internal Service Rules should track these model orders' definitions of misconduct and disciplinary procedure. This is why `11_SERVICE_RULES.md` exists.
- If HN crosses 50 workmen at any time (e.g. expansion), certification becomes mandatory within 6 months. Plan for this in advance — third outlet would push the count.

### 3.4 Payment of Wages Act 1936

**Sec 7(2)(c) and Sec 10** — deductions for damage or loss.
- Permitted only where: (a) the goods/cash were *expressly entrusted* to the employee for custody, AND (b) the loss is *directly attributable to neglect or default*.
- **Mandatory show-cause + opportunity to be heard** before deducting.
- Cap: deduction cannot exceed the actual loss. Total deductions in any wage period cannot exceed **50% of wages** (75% if including co-op society payments).

This is the *only* legal route to recover cash shortage from a cashier *without* taking him to civil court. Most restaurant owners screw this up by deducting unilaterally; that itself is an unfair labour practice.

### 3.5 Payment of Gratuity Act 1972

- 5 years' continuous service trigger.
- 15 days' wages × completed years.
- Payable even on **dismissal for misconduct**, with the narrow exception of Sec 4(6): forfeitable only if termination is for an act *constituting an offence involving moral turpitude committed in the course of employment*, or for *willful damage / loss / destruction of employer property*. Forfeiture must be invoked in the dismissal order and quantified to the extent of loss.
- Must be paid within **30 days** of becoming payable. Delay → 10% simple interest.

**Practical:** for fraud/theft dismissals, explicitly invoke Sec 4(6) in the dismissal order to forfeit gratuity. Don't just be silent — silence = gratuity payable.

### 3.6 Code on Wages 2019

Now operational in many states; Karnataka has notified the Karnataka Code on Wages Rules 2024. Section 17(2) requires **all wages payable on termination to be paid within 2 working days of the last working day**. The old "F&F in 30–45 days" practice is *technically* not compliant. Doesn't change the substantive law — just the timeline. Owner: pay F&F faster than people expect; this kills 80% of the early-stage friction that drives ex-employees to lawyers.

### 3.7 Karnataka Payment of Subsistence Allowance Act 1988

Triggered when an employee is **suspended pending enquiry**. Rates:
- First 90 days: **50% of wages**
- 91–180 days: **75%**
- Beyond 180 days: **90%**

Must be paid on the regular pay day. Failure to pay subsistence allowance vitiates the suspension — the workman is then deemed to have been on duty and entitled to full wages. (See *Bombay HC, Hindustan Lever Employees Union v HUL* — attendance not even required to claim subsistence.)

**Practical:** keep enquiries short. Every additional month of suspension costs more, and an unfinished enquiry past 6 months is a soft target for an "indefinite suspension is illegal" challenge (the *Shankar Yamanappa Talawar v CEO*, Karnataka HC 2025 principle — though that case concerned a public body, the reasoning is being extended).

## 4. Door 1 — Fraud / theft / dishonesty (misconduct dismissal)

### 4.1 The standard of proof

Domestic enquiries operate on the **preponderance of probabilities** standard (much lower than criminal "beyond reasonable doubt"). The leading restatement: *Union of India v. Sardar Bahadur* (1972 SC) — strict rules of Evidence Act do not apply, but principles of natural justice do.

This is the single biggest advantage the owner has: he doesn't need a criminal-court-grade case. He needs:
- A coherent, documented chain of evidence (CCTV time-stamps + cash recon + inventory variance + witness statement)
- A clear charge-sheet
- A fair enquiry where the employee got to defend
- A reasoned finding

### 4.2 Is a domestic enquiry mandatory?

**Default: yes, mandatory.** Under Standing Orders / Service Rules / KSE Act Sec 39 proviso, dismissal for misconduct without enquiry is *prima facie* invalid.

**Three escape routes (use only with caution):**

1. **Enquiry not "reasonably practicable"** — extremely narrow. Requires the employer to demonstrate it was impractical (e.g. employee absconding, threat of violence). The Supreme Court's March 2025 ruling on Article 311(2)(b) (govt service) reinforces that this is a high bar. For private employment, even narrower.

2. **Loss of confidence (without enquiry)** — for employees in **positions of special trust** (cashier, accountant, key-holder). Courts allow termination for loss of confidence *without* full enquiry, BUT the employer must justify the loss with **objective material on record**, and the Tribunal will hold a "para 3 enquiry" itself to test whether the loss of confidence was reasonably grounded. *Karnataka HC has affirmed this for theft cases* — Labour Court cannot reinstate "on grounds of sympathy" once theft by a person in trust is shown (recent Karnataka HC ruling on workman reinstatement, 2024).

3. **Enquiry held but found to be defective** — under Sec 11A of ID Act, the employer can lead evidence afresh before the Tribunal to justify the dismissal. So a botched enquiry is not always fatal. But this is plan B, not plan A.

**Practical:** for HN Hotels' typical case, plan for a **full enquiry** every time. The enquiry takes 2–4 weeks and ₹15K–₹40K (independent enquiry officer's fee). It is the cheapest insurance available.

### 4.3 The mandatory steps of a domestic enquiry (memorise this)

1. **Preliminary investigation** — within 24–48 hours of the incident. Collect CCTV, inventory log, recon sheet, witness statements. Preserve evidence.
2. **Suspension order** (optional but advisable for serious misconduct) — written, served, with subsistence allowance kicking in.
3. **Charge-sheet** served on employee — listing each charge with date, time, place, what was done, and which Standing Order / Service Rule clause it violates. Reasonable time to reply (24h, 48h, 72h, or 7 days depending on gravity — for theft, 48–72h is standard).
4. **Show-cause / explanation** received from employee.
5. **Decision to hold enquiry** — written order naming an Enquiry Officer (must be neutral; ideally an independent advocate, not the owner himself, and *never* the complainant or the owner's accountant who handles cash). Also a Presenting Officer who leads the management's case.
6. **Notice of enquiry** to employee — date, time, place, right to be assisted by a co-worker (not an outside lawyer unless Standing Orders permit).
7. **Enquiry hearing** — management leads evidence, witnesses examined, employee gets to cross-examine. Then employee's defence. All recorded in minutes.
8. **Enquiry Officer's report** — findings of fact on each charge, reasoned.
9. **Second show-cause** on proposed punishment (best practice; not strictly required after 1976 amendment to Sec 11A but still recommended in Karnataka).
10. **Disciplinary order** — speaking order recording charges, evidence, findings, punishment, and (if dismissal) explicit invocation of gratuity forfeiture clause.

**What makes findings "perverse" or "vitiated" (these are the killers):**
- Enquiry Officer is biased / had pre-judged
- Charge-sheet is vague ("misconduct" without specifics)
- Witnesses not produced for cross-examination
- Documents relied on but not shown to the employee
- Employee not given assistance from co-worker after requesting it
- Findings unsupported by any evidence on record
- Punishment grossly disproportionate to charge

### 4.4 Charge-sheet format requirements (and a minimum specimen)

Each charge must specify:
- **What** was done (precise act, not "misconduct")
- **When** (date and time)
- **Where** (which outlet, which counter)
- **Which rule** is violated (e.g. "Service Rule 5.2 — Theft of employer's property")
- **What evidence** will be produced
- **Time to reply** (e.g. "within 48 hours from receipt")
- **Consequences** of failing to reply (enquiry will proceed ex parte)

Vague language is the #1 reason charge-sheets fail. *Don't write*: "You have been found indulging in misconduct prejudicial to the interest of the company". *Do write*: "On 14 March 2026 at 21:42 hours, at the cashier counter of Hamza Express Shivajinagar, you removed ₹3,200 in cash from till transactions T-104, T-117, T-128 by issuing void receipts after customer had paid. CCTV camera 3 recording from 21:38–21:50 captures the act. The act constitutes theft of employer's property under Service Rule 5.2 and clause 14(3)(g) of the model Standing Orders."

A specimen for cashier theft is in `21_SOP_FRAUD_TERMINATION.md`. Adapt for: kitchen pilferage (specify items + quantity + location), repeated absenteeism (specify each date with proof of intimation/non-intimation), gross negligence (specify act + consequence + quantified loss).

### 4.5 Time limits

- **Charge-sheet** should ideally be served within **30 days** of the misconduct or its discovery. Longer delays must be explained in the charge-sheet itself ("the irregularity came to light on 12 April 2026 during quarterly reconciliation"). Karnataka HC accepts reasonable explanations; unexplained delays of 6+ months are vulnerable.
- **Show-cause reply window**: 48–72 hours for routine, up to 7–14 days for complex.
- **Enquiry completion**: target 30 days, hard limit 90 days. Beyond 90 days, suspension cost climbs to 75% wages and management has to explain the delay.

### 4.6 Suspension during enquiry

Permissible. Must be in writing. Subsistence allowance (Sec 3.7 above) kicks in from day 1. Suspension is *not* punishment — it is a protective interim measure to protect evidence and prevent intimidation of witnesses.

If the enquiry exonerates the employee, the period of suspension must be treated as on-duty with full back-wages.

### 4.7 Recovery of loss (the cash-shortage problem)

Even after dismissal, recovering the lost amount is a separate question:

- **Payment of Wages Act Sec 7(2)(c) + Sec 10** — yes, but only with show-cause heard, and capped at the loss (and at 50% of any single wage period). For losses larger than half a month's salary, deduction can only run instalments across several wage cycles — but if the employee has already been dismissed, there are no wage cycles left.
- **Civil suit for recovery of money** — only viable for losses >₹50K typically (court fees + lawyer + 2-year timeline).
- **Criminal complaint u/s 378/381 IPC** (theft by servant) — possible, but parallel criminal proceedings can complicate the disciplinary case. Karnataka practice: criminal complaint AFTER disciplinary dismissal is final, not before. And: criminal acquittal does *not* automatically displace dismissal. Disciplinary findings stand on a different (lower) standard. *(See ss 4.1 and the Karnataka HC ruling on theft + employer trust.)*
- **Gratuity forfeiture u/s 4(6) Gratuity Act** — recover up to the amount of loss, by forfeiting gratuity. Must be invoked in the dismissal order with quantified loss. This is the cleanest practical recovery vehicle for losses ≤ accumulated gratuity.
- **Final settlement adjustment** — owner can adjust against unpaid wages, leave encashment, gratuity (up to Sec 4(6) limit) as long as full and final settlement is signed by the employee with full disclosure. Get the employee to sign a written F&F with a recital of the loss being adjusted. Don't strong-arm; that creates a constructive-dismissal claim for the *amount taken*.

### 4.8 The owner's mistakes that blow up fraud cases (audit checklist)

- ❌ Verbal dismissal first, paperwork later. Always paperwork first.
- ❌ Owner himself acts as enquiry officer. Engage an independent advocate.
- ❌ "Resign or I file FIR" — constructive dismissal. Don't say it. Don't write it.
- ❌ Calling police before completing the enquiry (FIR can be used as enquiry evidence; police investigation should not stall enquiry).
- ❌ Charge-sheet drafted in WhatsApp / verbal communication. Always written, served, signed receipt.
- ❌ Letting the suspended employee continue to be in the WhatsApp group / have access to systems. Cut access on day 1 of suspension.
- ❌ Forgetting the second show-cause on quantum of punishment (this gets cured by Sec 11A Tribunal review, but creates avoidable risk).
- ❌ Silent on gratuity in the dismissal order. Always explicitly forfeit u/s 4(6) where applicable.
- ❌ Not paying subsistence allowance during suspension — vitiates everything.

## 5. Door 2 — Poor performance / inefficiency (termination simpliciter)

### 5.1 The legal architecture

Performance termination is **not "misconduct"** unless poor performance is *accompanied by* insubordination, willful neglect, or repeated warnings ignored — in which case it converts to misconduct and goes through Door 1.

Pure performance termination is **"termination for any reason whatsoever"** under Sec 2(oo) ID Act → falls within *retrenchment* → triggers **Sec 25F compensation** (if 240+ days served): one month notice or pay in lieu + 15 days × completed years compensation + Govt notice.

So the real choice is:
- **Option A**: short tenure (<240 days) → Sec 39 KSE Act notice (1 month) is enough; no retrenchment compensation.
- **Option B**: longer tenure (240+ days) → Sec 25F compliance even though the language used is "performance".
- **Option C**: convert to misconduct (insubordination/wilful neglect documented) → full Door 1 procedure but no Sec 25F compensation.

### 5.2 Performance Improvement Plan (PIP) — what it does and doesn't do

**PIPs have no statutory force in India.** They are an HR construct adopted from US/UK practice. Indian courts:
- Accept a PIP as **evidence of opportunity to improve** (good)
- Accept that documented PIP failure supports termination simpliciter (good)
- Do NOT treat PIP as a substitute for the natural-justice procedure where termination is stigmatic (bad surprise for owners)

A defensible PIP for an HN Hotels cashier or kitchen helper:
- **Specific, measurable goals** — e.g., "Cash variance not to exceed ₹100 per shift across any 7-day period", or "Order accuracy in kitchen above 95% based on returns log".
- **Defined window** — 30 days minimum, 60 days realistic, 90 days strong. Anything less than 30 days reads as a sham.
- **Written communication** — handed to employee, signed acknowledgement, witness present.
- **Weekly check-ins, written** — owner's notes of what was discussed, what was lacking.
- **Final review meeting** — written outcome.
- **Termination letter** — references the PIP, the failures, and *avoids stigma language*. Don't say "you are dishonest"; say "you did not meet the documented performance benchmarks despite a 60-day improvement window".

### 5.3 What courts have accepted as proof

- Attendance and punctuality records
- Customer complaint logs (with customer signatures or dated written records)
- Variance reports against measurable benchmarks
- Witness statements from peers or supervisors (corroborated, not solo)
- Repeated *written* warnings — at least 3, with gap of 15–30 days

What courts have rejected:
- Owner's verbal claim of poor performance with no contemporaneous record
- A single warning followed immediately by termination
- Termination labelled "performance" but motivated by personal animosity (court reads the WhatsApp messages → finds bad faith → reinstates)
- "Performance" termination during pregnancy / right after employee files a grievance (presumption of victimisation)

### 5.4 Minimum legal timeline

Realistic safe minimum, end to end:
- Day 0–7: First written observation of issue, oral counselling
- Day 7–30: First written warning
- Day 30–45: Second written warning
- Day 45–60: PIP issued in writing
- Day 60–105: PIP execution period (45 days minimum)
- Day 105–110: PIP review, termination decision
- Day 110: Termination letter with 30-day notice or pay in lieu + Sec 25F compensation if applicable

Faster than 60 days end-to-end → high risk of "sham PIP" finding.

### 5.5 Performance failure as misconduct?

Some Standing Orders and Service Rules list "habitual neglect of duty" or "gross inefficiency" as misconduct. Karnataka model standing orders include "habitual negligence" as misconduct under Schedule I.

This is the conversion bridge. If HN's Service Rules (Doc 11) define repeated unmet benchmarks as misconduct, the owner can route through Door 1 instead. Practical advantage: avoid Sec 25F compensation. Practical disadvantage: must run a full enquiry. Pick based on tenure — for 5+ year staff, the enquiry route saves more money than the compensation costs.

## 6. Probation — the underused tool

Probation is the **single biggest lever** an owner has, because during probation the protections of KSE Sec 39 (which need 6 months continuous service) and Sec 25F (which needs 240 days) are weaker.

### 6.1 Legal status

Probation is a contractual mechanism, not a statutory one. Indian courts treat it as a period during which the employer assesses suitability. **No notice required for non-confirmation** if the contract clearly says so and the termination is non-stigmatic.

### 6.2 Length and extension

- Standard: 3 to 6 months. HN should default to **6 months** for all hires.
- **Extension permitted**, but courts have struck down indefinite extensions:
  - *State of Punjab v. Dharam Singh* (1968 SC) — indefinite probation = deemed confirmation.
  - *V.P. Ahuja v. State of Punjab* (2000 SC) — silence beyond probation period = deemed confirmation.
  - Best practice: **at most one extension of 3 months, in writing, with reasons recorded**.

### 6.3 Termination during probation

- **Simpliciter** (no stigma) — short notice or even no notice if contract permits. Defensible. Courts will not interfere.
- **Stigmatic** (citing misconduct) — *Chaitanya Prakash v. H. Omkarappa* (2010 SC) — even probationer entitled to natural justice when termination involves stigma. So if the reason is "theft", run a mini-enquiry even during probation.
- **Form**: termination letter says "we are not satisfied with your performance during probation; your services are not confirmed and stand terminated with effect from [date]". Pay any earned dues. No 25F because <240 days.

### 6.4 What efflux of time does

If the owner does nothing at end of probation — no confirmation letter, no extension, no termination — and the employee continues to draw wages, the law will treat them as **deemed confirmed** for almost all protective purposes after 240 days of continuous service. Set a calendar reminder for every employee's 6-month and 12-month anniversaries.

## 7. Suspension, deductions, and recovery — practical mechanics

### 7.1 Suspension order — what to include

- Date, employee name, employee ID
- Reasons (charge under investigation)
- Effective date and time
- "You are not to enter the premises except for enquiry purposes"
- "You are not to communicate with other staff regarding the matter"
- Subsistence allowance rate cited (50% of last drawn wages for first 90 days)
- Designation of contact person for queries
- Signed by owner / authorised signatory; acknowledged by employee or sent via registered post + email if employee absconding

### 7.2 Subsistence allowance — calculation

Wages = basic + dearness allowance (DA), per the Karnataka 1988 Act definition. NOT including HRA, overtime, food allowance.

For a ₹25,000/month cashier: subsistence = ₹12,500/month for first 90 days. Pay through bank, not cash. Show in monthly muster.

### 7.3 Deductions for cash short / breakage / loss

- **Show-cause first** — "You are required to explain why ₹X should not be recovered from your wages for [specific incident dated DD/MM/YYYY]."
- **48-hour reply window** minimum.
- **Reasoned decision** — written order quantifying loss and direction to deduct.
- **Cap at actual loss**; cap deduction in any wage period at 50% of wages.
- **Spread over multiple cycles** if loss > one wage period's deduction cap.

### 7.4 Notice pay vs pay in lieu of notice

For workman with 6+ months service under KSE Sec 39: **1 month notice OR pay in lieu**. Pay in lieu = 1 month's wages, computed on basic + DA + fixed allowances paid monthly (NOT one-time bonuses or variable pay).

For Sec 25F retrenchment: same 1 month notice/pay PLUS 15 days × completed years compensation. These stack (the SC has confirmed the notice pay and retrenchment compensation are distinct heads).

## 8. Door 3 — Cost cutting / role redundancy (retrenchment)

### 8.1 When to use this door

Pure business decisions: outlet closure, role becoming obsolete (e.g., automation removes the need for a particular post), restructuring. There must be a **bona fide** business reason; no ulterior motive.

### 8.2 Section 25F mechanics — the four conditions precedent

For any workman with **240+ days continuous service**, before retrenchment:

1. **One month written notice** stating reasons, OR wages in lieu.
2. **Compensation of 15 days' average pay × completed years of continuous service** (any period >6 months counts as full year). "Average pay" = average of the 3 calendar months immediately preceding.
3. **Notice in prescribed form to the appropriate Government** (Karnataka: Form Q under the Karnataka Industrial Disputes Rules, served on the jurisdictional Asst. Labour Commissioner). The Form Q is a one-page form; serve by hand with acknowledgement, or registered post.
4. The non-compliance with point 3 alone has been held by the Bombay HC and various authorities to NOT automatically warrant reinstatement, but it gets the workman a strong procedural objection. Comply — the form is trivial.

### 8.3 Compensation calculation — worked example

Cashier with 4 years 8 months service, last drawn wages ₹22,000/month (basic + DA ~₹18,000):
- Completed years = 4. The 8 months part-year exceeds 6 months → counts as 1 more → **5 years**
- Average daily pay = ₹18,000 / 26 = ₹692
- 15 days × 5 years × ₹692 = **₹51,900 retrenchment compensation**
- + 1 month notice pay = ₹22,000
- Total severance: ~₹74,000

This is the floor. Add gratuity (since 5 years served): 15 × 18000 × 5 / 26 = ₹52,000. Plus accrued leave encashment, last salary.

### 8.4 Section 25G — Last In First Out (LIFO)

Within a category, the **last hired must be the first retrenched** unless the employer **records reasons in writing** for deviation.

"Category" = distinguishable role, not the entire workforce. So at HN, "kitchen helper" is one category, "cashier" is another, "waiter" is another. LIFO applies *within* each.

Reasons for deviation must be objective and recorded *before* the retrenchment decision: e.g., "the senior helper has unique tandoor expertise that the junior does not have; retaining her is essential". Without contemporaneous reasons, the deviation is assumed colourable.

### 8.5 Section 25H — Re-employment preference

For 12 months from date of retrenchment, if the employer hires for the same category, the **retrenched workman has preference**. Employer must offer the role first; if refused or not contactable (last known address), the obligation is discharged.

In practice, courts treat this as a soft obligation. Failure to comply gets the retrenched workman damages, not reinstatement. But still: keep contact details, send a registered letter when re-hiring.

### 8.6 Section 25-N — does NOT apply to HN Hotels

Sec 25-N (prior Govt permission for retrenchment) triggers at **100 workmen**. HN at 32 is well below. So HN does *not* need Govt permission to retrench. This is a major strategic advantage relative to a 150-employee competitor.

If HN ever crosses 99 workmen across both outlets (or any single establishment), Sec 25-N kicks in — Govt permission becomes mandatory and effectively halts retrenchment in the unorganised sector. *Plan around 95 employees as a soft ceiling.*

### 8.7 Voluntary Separation Scheme (VSS) / Voluntary Retirement Scheme (VRS)

Mechanism: offer employees a higher-than-statutory severance in exchange for voluntary resignation. Pros:
- Avoids Sec 25F litigation risk (no retrenchment, no LIFO, no Form Q)
- Creates reusable optics of fairness
- Tax-exempt up to **₹5,00,000** under Sec 10(10C) of Income Tax Act if the scheme is "in accordance with prescribed guidelines" (CBDT Rule 2BA — broadly, scheme must apply to a class of employees, completed at least 10 years service or 40 years of age, vacant post not to be filled, exemption availed only once per lifetime)
- Employee usually signs a **mutual separation agreement** with full and final waiver

Limits / risks:
- Indian courts permit VRS waivers but **scrutinise for unconscionability**. If VRS is offered under duress (e.g., "take VRS or you'll be retrenched at lower compensation"), courts may treat it as constructive dismissal. *X v. Registrar* (2022 SC) is the controlling authority.
- The waiver of statutory rights (gratuity, PF) is NOT permitted — VRS package must include them.
- For VRS to displace Sec 25F: the scheme must be *genuinely voluntary*. Single-employee "VRS offers" with no choice are routinely struck down. Make VRS a written scheme open to a defined class (e.g., "all kitchen helpers with >3 years service can opt by 30 May").

**Owner's practical use:** VRS is appropriate for restructuring with 5+ exits. For 1–2 exits, just do Sec 25F retrenchment cleanly.

### 8.8 The "sham retrenchment" trap

If HN retrenches a "redundant" cashier on Monday and hires a new cashier on Friday — the court will see through it and order reinstatement. Three rules to avoid this:
1. The post must genuinely cease to exist or be filled by re-deploying an existing employee.
2. No new hire in the same category for at least 6 months (12 months ideally — Sec 25H reemployment window).
3. Document the business case: declining sales, automation, closure of a section. Auditable financial proof.

## 9. Cross-cutting risks and doctrines

### 9.1 Constructive dismissal

Triggered when an employer's conduct effectively forces resignation:
- Forced resignation under threat of FIR / criminal complaint
- Salary cut without consent
- Demotion without disciplinary basis
- Transfer to a humiliating role
- Persistent harassment
- Stripping responsibilities to the point of marginalisation

Courts will treat the resignation as termination by employer → full Sec 25F (or even reinstatement) liability.

*X v. Registrar, MP High Court* (2022 SC) is the modern authority. *Suresh Kumar v. Sainik School Society* (HP HC) is also good Indian precedent.

**Owner's mistakes that trigger this:**
- "Sign this resignation letter or I'll dismiss you for theft"
- Cutting cashier's working days from 6 to 2 to push them to leave
- Moving experienced waiter to dishwashing as "punishment" without enquiry

### 9.2 Loss of confidence (the careful escape hatch)

Karnataka HC and Supreme Court accept that for employees in **positions of trust** (cashier, accountant, custodian of stock), loss of confidence is itself a ground for termination. But:
- The loss must be based on **objective facts on record**, not subjective gut feel.
- Even where enquiry is dispensed with, the Tribunal (under Sec 11A) will independently assess whether the loss of confidence was reasonably grounded.
- Quantum of theft / fraud is irrelevant; the breach of trust is the issue.

This doctrine is the **last-resort** escape from a botched enquiry, not a first-line strategy. If you have evidence, run the enquiry — don't shortcut to "loss of confidence" thinking it's easier.

### 9.3 Sec 11A — Tribunal can rewrite the punishment

Even with a clean enquiry, the Tribunal can substitute the employer's punishment with a lesser one (e.g., reinstate without back-wages, or reduce dismissal to suspension). Courts have used this for:
- Senior employee with long clean record, single incident
- Punishment "shocking the conscience" given the misconduct
- Trivial financial loss with disproportionate dismissal

So even a clean dismissal isn't bulletproof. Mitigation: punishment should be proportionate. For a ₹500 cash variance, dismissal of a 10-year cashier may be reduced to a written warning by the Tribunal. For a ₹50K systematic theft over 3 months, dismissal is upheld.

### 9.4 Sec 33 / 33A bar during pending disputes

If any industrial dispute is pending before a conciliation officer or Tribunal — even for a different employee — the employer cannot dismiss any workman concerned in the dispute *without the Tribunal's prior approval*. Approval applications are Form K under Karnataka rules. This is a major operational headache; avoid stacking terminations during disputes.

### 9.5 Restaurant / hotel-specific case law

Karnataka HC has decided several hotel/restaurant employee dismissal cases. Key principles emerging:
- Theft by cashier / store-keeper / waiter handling cash → dismissal upheld even on relatively low evidence, given trust nature of role.
- Pilferage of food / consumables by helper → dismissal upheld where inventory variance + witness corroboration.
- Single absent-without-leave incident → dismissal struck down as disproportionate; suspension or warning ordered instead.
- Habitual late attendance with documented warnings → dismissal upheld.
- Chef refusing to take orders from supervisor (insubordination) → dismissal upheld where standing orders define insubordination as misconduct.

The Federal HC, the Supreme Court in *Federation of Hotel and Restaurant Associations* line of cases, primarily address sector-level economic regulation, not individual employee dismissals — but they confirm the entire sector is squarely within ID Act / SE Act coverage.

## 10. Decision frameworks for the working business

### 10.1 The 5-question filter before any termination

1. **Is the employee a workman?** (95% yes for HN's cooks/helpers/waiters/cashiers/cleaners/tea masters.)
2. **What is the trigger — fraud, performance, or business need?** Determines which door.
3. **What is the tenure?** <6 months → KSE Sec 39 doesn't apply. <240 days → Sec 25F doesn't apply. >5 years → gratuity + reputational stakes high.
4. **Is there contemporaneous documentary evidence?** If no, don't fire today — build the file first.
5. **Is anyone watching?** (Pending dispute? Recent grievance? Pregnant? Just complained about wages?) — these convert ordinary terminations into "victimisation" claims.

### 10.2 Cost projection — cheapest to most expensive

Approximate cost (severance + legal exposure) for a 3-year-tenured cashier earning ₹25K/month:

| Path | Severance | Legal fees | Tribunal exposure | Total expected |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Resignation (genuine) | 0 | 0 | Low | ₹0 |
| Probation non-confirmation (within 6 months) | 0 | 0 | Low | ₹0 |
| KSE Sec 39 simpliciter (1 month notice pay) | ₹25K | ₹15K | Low-Med | ₹40–60K |
| Sec 25F retrenchment (1mo + 15d×3) | ₹25K + ₹35K | ₹15K | Med | ₹75K–1L |
| Misconduct dismissal with clean enquiry | 0 | ₹40K | Med | ₹40–80K |
| Misconduct dismissal botched | 0 → reinstated + back-wages | ₹2L | High | ₹4–6L |
| Constructive dismissal / forced resignation | 0 → reinstated | ₹2L | Very High | ₹6–10L |

Botched fraud terminations are the worst possible outcome. They are also the most common.

### 10.3 The minimum compliance checklist before any termination letter

- [ ] Written contract / appointment letter on file
- [ ] Written Service Rules / Standing Orders applicable, with employee signed acknowledgement
- [ ] PF / ESI registration current
- [ ] Wages paid till date
- [ ] Attendance records reconciling
- [ ] Performance log / charge documentation in writing
- [ ] (Misconduct) Show-cause served and replied; enquiry held; report on file
- [ ] (Performance) PIP issued and run for ≥45 days
- [ ] (Retrenchment) Sec 25F notice/compensation calculated and ready; Form Q drafted
- [ ] Termination letter drafted (no stigma language for performance/retrenchment)
- [ ] Gratuity calculated; forfeiture explicitly invoked if applicable
- [ ] F&F sheet prepared for payment within 2 working days
- [ ] Returned items log (uniform, ID card, keys)
- [ ] Final clearance certificate ready

### 10.4 When to call a labour-law advocate (not optional)

- Loss > ₹50,000 in fraud cases
- Any retrenchment of 2+ workmen at one time
- Any termination during pending dispute
- Any termination of an employee with >5 years service
- Any employee on maternity leave or recently returned
- Any complaint already filed by the employee
- Any case where employee's union/lawyer has already written

For routine probation non-confirmation and clean Sec 39 simpliciter terminations of non-workman supervisors, advocate involvement is a luxury, not a need.
